The Computer and Video Game Industry

The Video Game Industry is a subcategory of the Entertainment Industry. Talented individuals come together to create a product for the masses to enjoy and be entertained by. Much like movies, games go through similar phases of production. Scheduling the initial roadmap, budgeting, pre-production and concepting, early prototyping, ramping up hiring professionals and services, production and development, testing and reviewing, post-production and polish, marketing, release, ramp down. Unlike movies, games enter a sustainment period, providing updates, bug fixes, and additional content. For live-service games, the sustainment period is intended to be indefinite.

Budgets and success of games varies widely. Often, games studios funded by big publishers see widespread success. Sometimes large productions will fail and publishers have to cut their losses. Independently developed games with little to no budgets have seen both massive commercial success. While some indie games with millions of dollars are commercial flops. Or maybe game sales or player-base is small at first, but slowly ramp up overtime as the game is updated and word of mouth spreads.

Games are sold on a variety of devices. Serious Games is often considered to be games sold on PC and Console platforms. Casual Games are games distributed on mobile devices or on web-platforms.

Games are either released for free or at a set price tag. The game is then supplemented with either free or paid downloadable content. The games-as-service model includes various mechanisms to entice players to continually purchase paid DLC.

In 2018, the Game Industry achieved a global market value of $134 Billion dollars. It brings in more revenue than Film ($41B), Streaming Video on Demand ($22B), and Music ($19B). Gaming makes up a decent chunk of the entertainment industry.

The Games Industry is broken up into 3 markets. Mobile (47%), PC (25%), and Console (28%).

Extra Reality Games (XR) are broken up into two subcategories, Virtual Reality (VR) and Augmented Reality (AR). AR presents virtual world on a 3D space using a device’s camera. The most notable AR game is Pokemon Go. VR lets the user see a virtual world from a first person perspective using a stereoscopic head-mounted display.

How to Get a Job in the Games Industry

In the Game Industry, studios are interested in hiring talented professionals to make a commercial product. The development team’s goal is to make a game that is fun and enjoyable. The business team’s goal is to reach as many consumers as possible and to turn a profit from game sales, downloadable content, and merchandising. Games take years to make, so there is always risk when bringing on new people mid-development.

Making games is a business, so studios want to minimize risk and maximize productivity. A majority of the vacancies in industry are for mid-level and senior-level positions. There is less incentive for studios to hire interns or junior staff because training costs time and resources. Studios want you to already have the skills necessary to do the work that needs to be done. They will not hire someone that cannot prove they are competent for a role.

Along with a lack of internships and junior positions, more and more individuals are studying game development as an education or a hobby. Therefore, there is far more competition for these entry level positions. For example, the acceptance rate for an internship at Blizzard Entertainment is less than 1%. So you have to make your application standout from the rest.

Recruiters are looking for individuals that meet the job requirements, and can make personal connections. They will toss out any job application that doesn’t have a genuine cover letter and a portfolio of your past works. Describe your abilities or aptitude, talk about your personal experiences, and make a connection as to how your skills would be a good fit for the job. You want to portray yourself as the solution to their problem.

In your portfolios, show that you can program the systems that the studio needs programmed. Show that you can make art in the same art style and quality as the studio. Show that you can manage a project under a tight schedule with great organization and detail. Show that you can market games to a target audience. Show that you can manage a community and have excellent communication skills. Show that you can test games and find bugs.

If you currently do not have the education or skills to fulfill a developer or business role but still want to work in the Games Industry, you can work in other entry level roles. These include game testing, customer service, IT desktop support, and various facility roles.

Another avenue is to attend conventions. Conventions give industry professionals and fans the opportunity to come together and mingle in one place. Most conventions, such as PAX and E3, are catered more towards the fans and the marketing of upcoming games. Yet developers are present and you do have the chance to talk with them. There are also a few networking events that are held during the same week as the conventions. Your best bet for career networking in the Game Industry is at GDC, the Game Developers Conference. Companies are actively seeking to recruit individuals and do interviews on the spot. You will want to put your best foot forward and present yourself as the best candidate for and open postion.

Working in Game Testing

Game testing is an entry level position where your job is to test daily builds of a game for bugs. Often you will perform general ad-hoc of certain game features, or run specific test cases to verify functionality. Sometimes, testers will be asked to perform playtests and provide feedback on the current state of the game, providing incite and suggestions. Occasionally, testers are given the opportunity to make tools for the project, or perform a more in-depth investigation into critical issues.

If any bugs are found, then the bugs are submitted to a bug tracking database. Producers will triage new bugs, quickly assessing whether a bug is valid, and sending it to the appropriate dev team to be fixed. Once a bug is fixed, it gets sent back to the testers to verify the fix was implemented. If a bug is verified to be fixed by a tester, then the test managers will close out the bugs. However if a tester finds that a bug is still occuring, then the test managers will send the bug back to the dev team that worked on the fix.

All game testers are required to sign a Non-Disclosure Agreement. By signing the NDA, the Tester agrees to not discuss the game they are working on. If details of a game are leaked and the culprit is discovered, the tester will be reprimanded and barred from working at the studio in the future.

Testers also perform Compliance testing in order to approve a Release Candidate build of the game.  the Release Candidate is the game build that meets all the compliance requirements, is free of any critical bugs, and will be version of the game available on the Release Date. Compliance testing is done to make sure the game meets the strict technical requirements of First-Party console platforms, and that the game will meet the targeted game rating issued by a Ratings Board. Sony’s Playstation, Microsoft’s Xbox, and Nintendo systems each have their own guidelines for legal, technical, and performance requirements.  Ratings boards, such as the ESRB and PEGI, each describe what content is suitable for various age groups.

Compliance verification is not free and the approval process takes time. So if a game fails compliance, it can cause delays in publishing and releasing the game, along with additional cost for repeat compliance testing and verification. When the game passes compliance, The Release Candidate is approved and will be the version of the game to launch on the release date. A copy of the build is sent to manufacturers to be printed on disks and shipped out to retail stores. A copy is also uploaded to digital distribution platforms as well.

With the advent of digital downloads, games will often ship with major bugs that couldn’t be fixed before the game needed to be sent off for publishing. So development and testing will continue  so that a patch can be sent out and downloaded at launch to fix these issues.

Depending on a tester’s skills, aptitude, and career interests, they have the opportunity to pursue careers as a test manager, producer, developer, or community manager role.

The Downside of Game Testing

The most obvious downside to game testing is the pay. Studios tend to pay game testers minimum wage, because it is considered to be a low skill profession. Studios need as many people to manually test the game and to find as many bugs as possible. Often times, game testers are hired as a temporary contract workers instead of a full-time employee. So not only you don’t receive the same benefits as full employees, you don’t have the same job security as they do. As a result, game testers end up making the rounds from studio to studio in areas like the bay area to keep a steady living. Even though your work in quality assurance is critical to the game’s quality and shippability, you are the least valued member of the team and are often the first to be let go when the studio ramps down production.

Sometimes the testers and developers don’t always get along. Developers may feel attacked when testers submit a bug or give feedback. Passion for the project can fog judgement and result in a lack of professionalism. Tensions rise due to miscommunication or misinterpretation, stress is high trying to meet impending deadlines, and poor decision making from lack of sleep and over working. In studios where the testers don’t interact with the developers on a daily basis, testers can feel like they are isolated and not a part of the team. Concerns brought up by the testing team can often go ignored by the development team for long periods of time. Ultimately, these issues can be a detriment to the quality of the game if they do not get addressed.

Game studios have the notion is that “anyone can be a game tester”. As long as they can hold a controller, record media, and type on a keyboard, you can be a game tester. But quality assurance suffers because of the lack of experience. This will lead to incompetence with PC and dev kit usage, inability to troubleshoot common tech issues, misinterpretation of game functionality, inefficient testing procedures, poor bug writing, mismanaged database, incoherent spreadsheets and reports, and unprofessional communication. The amount of training and support a QA tester receives varies from studio to studio.

Culture in the Games Industry

There has been a lot of articles written recently about the toxic work culture in the Game Industry.

Studios are taking advantage of developer’s passions and encouraging unpaid overtime. Management and peers pressure developers to participate. If you don’t, then are looked down on for not being passionate enough. Also, if you choose not to work unpaid overtime, you are more likely to be let go when the project ramps down. Crunch time lasts for a couple weeks to several months and can heavily impact work-life balance. Constantly working for 50 to 100 hrs a week can lead to fatigue from lack of sleep and mental breakdowns from heavy stress. Eventually, game developers burnout and leave the industry.

Only 22% of developers are women, yet those that are present have to put up with sexual harassment at studios that exhibit bro culture. Nobody should have to deal with the problems female employees faced like at Riot Games and Rockstar Games.

Developers and testers can also run into a lot of frustrations. Especially when the contracted or external and rarely interacts with the development team. This can lead to feelings of isolation and resentment as with testers at Treyarch.

Some studios have a tendency to let go of staff without much warning. It takes time to find a new job and get hired, so being let go on such short notice can leave you financially insecure for an unexpected period of time, like at Telltale Games. Also, businesses are always open to reducing the bottom line, even when a company is making record revenue. Large swaths of staff are fired for restructuring only for the company to rehire new employees for lower pay, as with Activision Blizzard. Game Testers often are brought on enmasse and let go after 9 to 12 months. Often, companies list contract positions because they don’t have to provide benefits and don’t have to rehire them after the alloted period, like with Electronic Arts and Epic Games.

All these issues in the Game Industry have led many to believe that now is the time to push for unionization.

Unionizing the Game Industry

(Talk about the reasons why game workers should unionization and the results so far)

In order to combat the issues of unpaid overtime, job insecurity, unfair wages, discrimination, and sexual harassment, a grassroots advocacy group started called Game Workers Unite. GWU’s goal is to encourage game industry workers around the world to unionize.

Unionization will allow Game Industry workers to take collective action in negotiating work hours, pay scales, and benefits with their employers. Union representatives can also help workers to take legal action for underpayment, harassment, and wrongful termination.

In the UK, Game Workers Unite UK became the first union in the Game Industry as a branch of the Independent Workers Union of Great Britain (IWGB). Their purpose is to fight for better working conditions and benefits, provide legal assistance, organize strike against employers, end the practice of Crunch, improve diversity and inclusion, and to stop any toxic behavior. The benefits greatly outway the costs, and will bring about a healthier environment for game developers.
